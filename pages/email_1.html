Technical Terms Glossary

  • API Key (Application Programming Interface Key)

    A unique, secret code that one application uses to identify and authenticate itself with another. It's a password, but for software instead of people. Think of it like this: An API Key is like a highly secure, digital key card for a hotel. Your popup plugin (the guest) uses its specific key card to open the door to your email service's data (the hotel room). The hotel‚Äôs front desk (the API) only grants access if the key card is valid.

  • Integration

    The process of connecting two or more separate software applications so that they can automatically share data and work together. Think of it like this: An integration is like hiring a professional translator. Your website speaks "WordPress," and your email service speaks "ConvertKit." The integration (like the Brave plugin) translates the "new signup" message, allowing them to have a seamless conversation.

  • Plugin / Extension

    A smaller piece of software that adds a new feature or functionality to a larger existing program (like WordPress or a web browser). Think of it like this: A plugin is like an attachment for a vacuum cleaner. The vacuum (your website) works fine on its own, but if you want to clean a specific spot (like create a popup), you attach a special tool (the plugin) to get the job done.

  • Lead Magnet

    A free, valuable piece of content or an offer you give someone in exchange for their email address. Think of it like this: A lead magnet is like the free cheese samples at a grocery store. The store offers you a delicious, no-risk taste (the free checklist or ebook) in the hopes that you'll like it and decide to become a regular shopper (a subscriber).

  • Opt-in / Consent

    The explicit, verifiable permission someone gives you to send them emails. This is a legal and ethical requirement (part of laws like GDPR and CAN-SPAM). Think of it like this: Getting opt-in consent is like asking someone for their phone number before you call them. You wouldn't just find random numbers and start dialing. The person willingly gives you their number (email) and expects you to call (send emails).

  • Automation / Flow / Journey

    A pre-built path of actions that are automatically triggered by a specific event. Different companies use different names, but they all mean the same thing. Think of it like this: An automation is like a set of dominoes. You set them all up just once. Then, all you have to do is tip the first one (the trigger), and the rest will fall in sequence exactly as you planned.

  • Trigger

    The starting event that kicks off an automation. The "if this..." part of an "if this, then that" statement. Think of it like this: It's the starting pistol for a race. For example: *If a person signs up for my list...* (that's the trigger), *...then send them a welcome email.*

  • Action

    A single step or task that is performed within an automation. The "...then that" part of the statement. Think of it like this: It's what your system *does* after the trigger occurs. Common actions include "Send an email," "Wait for 3 days," or "Add a tag to the contact."

  • Call-to-Action (CTA)

    The primary action you want the reader to take, usually presented as a button or a link. Think of it like this: A CTA is like a clear, well-lit "EXIT" sign in a building. It removes all guesswork and directs people exactly where you want them to go next. An email without a clear CTA is like a hallway with no doors.

  • Transactional Email

    An automated email sent to an individual in response to a specific action they took (e.g., password resets, shipping confirmations). Think of it like this: It's like the receipt a cashier hands you after you buy something. It's not a marketing flyer; it's a functional confirmation of an action you just took, which is why these emails are opened so often.
